Why Co-ownership?

Purchasing a home with others is a practical, modern path to financial independence and community-centric living.

Immediate Affordability

Pool your deposits and buying power to afford a larger home, better neighborhoods, or lower monthly payments than you could alone.

Build Shared Equity

Redirect money away from rental landlords and into monthly equity accumulation, sharing the property appreciation over time.

Shared Responsibilities

Divide the load of home repairs, property taxes, insurance, and daily maintenance dynamically to make owning stress-free.

The Co-ownership Journey

Steps to successfully purchase and manage a shared home, ensuring clear expectations and peace of mind.

1 Align Goals

Partner selection and financial matching. Have transparent discussions about budgets, credit standings, and long-term plans for the property.

2 Draft Legal Agreements

Establish a legal co-ownership agreement detailing equity split, exit strategies (e.g. if someone wants to sell), and rules for hosting guests or renting rooms.

3 Secure Financing

Apply for a joint mortgage. Many lenders support fractional co-ownership loans, or joint tenancy mortgages where all buyers are listed.

4 Buy and Manage

Finalize the closing, move in, and set up a joint bank account for expenses to automate bills, utilities, and emergency maintenance reserves.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to common questions surrounding home co-ownership legalities and logistics.

You should draft a custom Co-ownership Agreement (or Tenants-in-Common Agreement) with a real estate attorney. This documents each person's equity stake, who handles maintenance bills, how properties are managed, and what happens if someone wants to move out or sell their portion.

Most groups get a standard joint mortgage. This means you are "jointly and severally liable," meaning the lender holds everyone responsible for the full balance. If one partner defaults, the others are legally responsible for the mortgage payments. Some specialized lenders offer fractional loans, but joint mortgages are the most common.

An exit strategy is the most crucial part of your legal contract. Usually, the agreement grants the remaining owners a "right of first refusal" to buy out the departing partner's equity based on an independent valuation. If they cannot or do not want to buy them out, the agreement usually outlines timelines to list the home on the open market.

Co-owners typically choose to hold title as Tenants in Common (TIC) rather than Joint Tenants. TIC allows you to hold unequal shares of the home (e.g. 50%, 30%, and 20%) matching your down payment split, and allows you to pass your share to heirs in a will rather than it automatically transferring to the other co-owners.
