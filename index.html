Co-Own a Better Way to Secure your Own Home

Dear Family & Friends,

We started exploring this approach because the traditional path to buying a home in today's market often forces people to settle for far less than they deserve.

Jared has been investing in Utah real estate with our family since 2017 (managing properties in Heber, Herriman, and Ogden, and helping guide a 64-unit community development in Heber). These experiences showed us just how powerful real estate is for building long-term security. Now, we want to bring those same wealth-building principles to our own housing, sharing the benefits with those we care about.

When we looked at the numbers for buying a home, we realized that the smartest and most supportive path forward is a structured, 5-year co-ownership where two households can lift each other up and get both families into their own homes.

This page is an introduction to how this co-ownership works. It outlines the numbers, the daily logistics, the legal protections, and our 5-year exit plan. We invite you to look through it, play with the numbers, and see if we can build something great together.

The Strategy: Combining Our Buying Power

We don't have to wait decades or compromise on safety and comfort to own a home we love. By pooling our resources, we can step into a beautiful, move-in-ready home in a great neighborhood right now, bypassing the competitive and overpriced starter-home market.

This works because of a simple market reality: home quality does not increase at the same rate as the price. In Utah's current market, entry-level single-family homes (around $375,000) are in the highest demand, meaning buyers pay a premium for properties that are often older, smaller, or need significant repairs.

If we combine our budgets to target a $750,000 home, the value we get is far more than double. We gain access to much larger layouts, higher-quality construction, and quieter neighborhoods. While a massive pool of buyers fights over the cheapest entry-level properties, larger homes with built-in private suites (Downstairs Apartments) remain much more reasonably priced. Together, we can buy a better home, on better terms, without the bidding wars.

The Foundation of Growth: Owning the Land

A classic principle of real estate is that buildings depreciate in value while the land appreciates in value. To build real long-term equity, owning the land is key. This is why townhomes and condos, which share land ownership and have high monthly fees, rarely build wealth as effectively as a single-family home on a dedicated lot.

HOAs: Understanding the Real Costs

When comparing a single-family home to a townhome or condo, Homeowner Association (HOA) fees are often overlooked. In reality, they are a significant drain on your monthly budget and long-term borrowing power.

Reduced Borrowing Power

Banks approve loans based on your total monthly payment. A typical $195/month HOA fee eats into your budget, reducing your mortgage borrowing power by roughly $25,000 to $30,000. You pay the same monthly amount, but own less actual property.

Zero Equity Return

Every dollar spent on HOA fees is gone forever. Over 5 years, a $195/month fee adds up to $11,700 in pure expense. That is money that could have been going directly toward paying down our mortgage and building real home equity.

No Control Over Costs

HOA boards manage community funds but often lack the incentive to minimize costs. Homeowners frequently face rising fees due to inefficient vendor contracts, management overhead, or neighborhood projects you might not agree with.

Unpredictable Fee Hikes

While a fixed-rate mortgage keeps your principal and interest payments predictable, HOA fees can rise at any time. Boards can increase monthly dues or issue sudden, expensive "special assessments" to cover underfunded community repairs.

By choosing a single-family home with no HOA, we keep full control over our expenses and make sure our hard-earned money builds our own future.

Dual Living: Space and Privacy Built-In

This isn't about living together as roommates. It is a structured property co-ownership designed to help both households build equity while enjoying complete space and privacy.

We will focus on homes that naturally offer two separate, fully private living areas—typically a main level home and a fully finished daylight basement (Downstairs). Each household will have:

  • Private Entrances: Entirely separate doorways, ensuring complete privacy.
  • Complete Amenities: Each home must have its own full kitchen, laundry room, bedrooms, bathrooms, and living areas.

To keep things running smoothly and fairly, we will divide the property duties into two clear roles:

  • The Financial Administrator handles joint billing, mortgage payments, taxes, and utility tracking, providing clear digital receipts.
  • The Property Manager oversees seasonal maintenance, landscaping, HVAC checks, and coordinates any necessary repair quotes.

Daily Operations Tech

These day-to-day duties will be handled professionally using automated expense routing, clear digital dashboards, and transparent digital receipt tracking. By relying on established tech infrastructure, we can manage the logistics seamlessly and completely avoid the friction of messy group texts.

Ideal basement apartments have independent private entrances, daylight window layouts, and completely separate living zones.

Simple, Fair Math: The Rent Equalization

We choose our overall ownership split at the start (such as 50/50 or 60/40), which determines our share of the down payment and joint expenses. But since the upstairs is larger and more valuable than the downstairs suite, how do we make the living arrangement completely fair?

To keep things balanced, a local real estate professional will estimate the fair market rent for both spaces. Using those numbers, we calculate a monthly Rent Equalization adjustment. The family living in the larger upstairs pays this adjustment to the family in the downstairs suite. This ensures that the total value each family receives—combining their living space and any cash adjustments—perfectly aligns with their ownership share.

Safeguards: Building a Strong, Trusted Co-ownership

Buying a home together is a big step. In any co-ownership, the greatest risks are unstated expectations. We want to align on all the details on paper first, ensuring complete peace of mind before we begin.

The Good Neighbor Agreement

Before we move in, we'll collaboratively document daily household logistics to keep our neighborhood relationship positive and clear:

  • Driveway Parking: Pre-allocated parking spaces and visitor guidelines.
  • Yard & Upkeep: Clear division of seasonal chores like mowing, weeding, and snow removal.
  • Pet Guidelines: Respectful boundaries for pet play areas and immediate waste clean-up.
  • Quiet Hours: Agreed quiet times during weekdays and weekend mornings.

Clear Legal Operating Agreement

A formal contract drafted by a real estate attorney to protect everyone's investments and clarify our exit options:

  • Financial Transparency: Mutual credit and income validation to secure optimal financing.
  • Payment Safety Net: If a family faces a temporary hardship, the other can step in to protect the mortgage, receiving a pre-agreed interest adjustment.
  • Default Protections: To prevent foreclosure, if a family defaults repeatedly, a formal buyout process is triggered.
  • Buyout Protocol: Legally defined appraisal procedures, ownership percentages, and timeline triggers.

Frequently Asked Questions & Exit Scenarios

Clear, direct answers on how we handle changing life circumstances and the 5-year term exit.

To resolve this, we use the Valuation Buyout Protocol (sometimes called "buy-cut-choose"). One family suggests a single total valuation for the home. The other family then has the absolute right to either buy out the first family's share at that price, or sell their own share to them at that same price. Because the person setting the price doesn't know which side of the deal they'll end up on, they are naturally incentivized to name a perfectly fair, market-accurate number.

The staying family will buy out the departing family's equity share based on a fair market valuation (established by a professional third-party appraisal). The leaving family receives their full equity share (their down payment, plus their share of appreciation and mortgage principal paid off) in cash, giving them a clean, funded launch into their next home.

To make sure the departing family transitions smoothly, if they have maintained a perfect payment history over the 5 years, the staying family agrees to help them qualify for their new home purchase by serving as a co-signer if needed. The staying family can leverage their own income and the rental value of the newly vacant suite to help the departing family secure great financing for their next home.

We will hold the title as Tenants in Common (TIC). Unlike joint tenancy, a Tenants in Common deed allows us to record unequal ownership shares (such as 60/40) to match our exact financial contributions. It also ensures that each family retains complete control over their share, allowing them to pass it on to heirs in their will.
